# Research: MinkowskiReduction.jl — Algorithm, Precision, and Non-Uniqueness

This document records a careful reading of the 3D Minkowski reduction code in
`src/MinkowskiReduction.jl`, together with background research on the
underlying theory (Minkowski 1896/1910; Nguyen & Stehlé, *Low-Dimensional
Lattice Basis Reduction Revisited*, ANTS-VI 2004, LNCS 3076, pp. 338–357) and
on the finite-precision pitfalls of low-dimensional lattice reduction. The
discussion is restricted to three dimensions, which is the only case this
package targets.

---

## 1. Problem statement

A lattice L ⊂ ℝ³ generated by three linearly independent basis vectors
{U,V,W} has infinitely many bases related by unimodular (integer,
determinant ±1) transformations. Among them, a **Minkowski-reduced** basis
is one whose vectors are, in a precise sense, as short as possible:

- ‖U‖ is the length λ₁ of the shortest non-zero lattice vector;
- ‖V‖ is the length λ₂ of the shortest lattice vector linearly independent
  from U;
- ‖W‖ is the length λ₃ of the shortest lattice vector linearly independent
  from {U,V}.

These three lengths are the **successive minima** of L. A classical theorem
(Minkowski, and later Nguyen–Stehlé 2004) says that in dimensions ≤ 4 a
Minkowski-reduced basis exists, achieves all three successive minima
simultaneously, and minimizes the orthogonality defect
δ(U,V,W) = ‖U‖‖V‖‖W‖ / |det(U,V,W)|.

In higher dimensions this convenient identity breaks — the greedy algorithm
can fail to find even λ₁ from dimension 5 onwards. But that is irrelevant
here: the code is a three-dimensional reducer only.

---

## 2. The algorithm in the code

The implementation follows the greedy algorithm of Nguyen and Stehlé (2004).
It has three layers.

### 2.1 `GaussReduce(U, V)` — the 2D engine

This is the classical Gauss–Lagrange two-dimensional reduction. The main
loop (line 91) is

```julia
V, U = U, V - round((U⋅V)/(U⋅U))*U
```

i.e. subtract the nearest integer multiple of U from V, then swap. The
coefficient `round((U⋅V)/(U⋅U))` is the rounded Gram–Schmidt projection: the
integer μ that minimises ‖V − μU‖.

Termination (line 93): the loop exits when `norm(U) > norm(V)` **or**
`norm(U) ≈ norm(V)`. On exit the pair (V, U) is returned with V as the
shorter vector. The `≈` branch is load-bearing — that is, it looks like
a redundant tolerance check but the algorithm does not terminate without
it. If `‖U‖ = ‖V‖` exactly, the strict `>` is false and the loop body
runs: it subtracts a rounded multiple of U from V (which, at exact
equality, may subtract zero or may swap which vector is "shorter") and
then swaps labels. The next iteration is back in the same configuration
with the roles of U and V interchanged, and the process oscillates
forever without making progress. The equal-norm configuration is not
exotic — it occurs in every highly symmetric 2D sublattice (the
in-plane lattice of FCC, BCC, hexagonal, tetragonal, and cubic Bravais
lattices all produce it after one step of 3D reduction). The `≈` clause
breaks the tie by accepting exact equality as a valid termination state.
The two possible "which-is-U-which-is-V" outputs at equal norms are both
correct Gauss-reduced pairs; the choice is one of the non-uniquenesses
discussed in §4.

The NaN guard on line 94 catches the case in which U and V are
numerically parallel (dot-product ratios blow up, norms become NaN).

On exit, the pair satisfies the 2D Minkowski conditions
‖V‖ ≤ ‖U±V‖ and ‖U‖ ≤ ‖V‖.

### 2.2 `shortenW_in_UVW(U, V, W)` — the 3D step

This is the core Nguyen–Stehlé construction (the paper passage cited in the
docstring). The idea: project W onto the plane spanned by U and V, then
shift W by integer multiples of U and V so that the tail of the projection
lies in a fundamental parallelogram of the (U,V)-lattice. If U and V are
already Gauss-reduced, this parallelogram's Voronoi cell is contained in it,
and so the lattice point of the (U,V)-sublattice closest to the projection
of W is one of the four corners of that parallelogram. Checking those four
corners (line 61) gives the shortest W modulo the (U,V)-sublattice.

The projection coefficients are computed algebraically:

```julia
denom = (U⋅U)(V⋅V) − (U⋅V)²        # = |U|²|V|² sin²θ — Gram determinant
a = floor( ((U⋅W)(V⋅V) − (V⋅W)(U⋅V)) / denom )
b = floor( ((V⋅W)(U⋅U) − (U⋅W)(U⋅V)) / denom )
```

The use of `floor` (rather than `round`) is deliberate: `floor` produces the
lower-left integer corner of the parallelogram containing W, and the
subsequent four-corner test chooses among {(0,0),(1,0),(0,1),(1,1)}
relative to that corner.

Correctness note. The four corners of the parallelogram are precisely
the integer combinations n₁U + n₂V with n₁, n₂ ∈ {0,1}, so (relative to the
"first-quadrant" W returned after the `floor` shift) the candidate closest
lattice points are W, W−U, W−V, W−U−V. For a Gauss-reduced (U,V), the
Voronoi hexagon of the 2D sublattice is contained inside the parallelogram
[0,1)² · (U,V), and its six neighbours are {±U, ±V, ±(U+V)} (if U,V have an
obtuse angle) or {±U, ±V, ±(U−V)} (if acute). The four-corner test
therefore covers all Voronoi neighbours of the candidate W.

### 2.3 `minkReduce(U, V, W)` — the outer loop

```julia
while true
    sort (U,V,W) by ascending norm
    U,V,W = shortenW_in_UVW(U,V,W)
    i > 15 && error(...)
    norm(W) ≥ norm(V) ≥ norm(U) && break
end
```

The convergence criterion is subtle. After `shortenW_in_UVW`, U and V are
Gauss-reduced and W is shortest in its affine plane. If additionally
‖W‖ ≥ ‖V‖ ≥ ‖U‖, then no further work can improve the basis — sorting
would leave the vectors in place, and the next call to `shortenW_in_UVW`
would re-Gauss-reduce U,V (a no-op) and recompute the same W. The
algorithm terminates.

Progress is guaranteed because each non-trivial iteration strictly decreases
‖U‖² + ‖V‖² + ‖W‖². Since this quantity is bounded below by λ₁² + λ₂² + λ₃²,
termination is assured. Nguyen and Stehlé show that for integer inputs of
bit-size B, the total number of iterations is O(B), giving quadratic
bit-complexity. The pathological `DeviousMat` family exercises this:
`DeviousMat(26)` — the largest size that does not overflow `Int64` —
requires exactly 15 iterations.

The hard-coded cap `i > 15` reflects this empirical worst case.

## 3. The 12 Minkowski conditions (as checked by `isMinkReduced`)

For a basis (U,V,W) with ‖U‖ ≤ ‖V‖ ≤ ‖W‖ to be Minkowski-reduced in 3D it
is necessary and sufficient that eleven further inequalities hold (Minkowski;
Tammela 1973). The code enumerates them as twelve conditions (conditions 1–2
being the two ordering inequalities; conditions 3–12 the ten reduction
inequalities). They can be grouped as follows:

| # | Inequality             | Meaning                         | Guaranteed by                                |
|---|------------------------|---------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| 1 | ‖U‖ ≤ ‖V‖              | ordering                        | sort step in `minkReduce`                    |
| 2 | ‖V‖ ≤ ‖W‖              | ordering                        | sort step in `minkReduce`                    |
| 3 | ‖V‖ ≤ ‖U+V‖            | V not reducible by −U           | `GaussReduce`                                 |
| 4 | ‖V‖ ≤ ‖U−V‖            | V not reducible by +U           | `GaussReduce`                                 |
| 5 | ‖W‖ ≤ ‖U+W‖            | W not reducible by −U           | four-corner step (corner 2)                  |
| 6 | ‖W‖ ≤ ‖U−W‖            | W not reducible by +U           | four-corner step (corner 2)                  |
| 7 | ‖W‖ ≤ ‖V+W‖            | W not reducible by −V           | four-corner step (corner 3)                  |
| 8 | ‖W‖ ≤ ‖V−W‖            | W not reducible by +V           | four-corner step (corner 3)                  |
| 9 | ‖W‖ ≤ ‖U+V+W‖          | W not reducible by −(U+V)       | four-corner step (corner 4)                  |
| 10| ‖W‖ ≤ ‖U−V+W‖          | W not reducible by V−U          | four-corner step (corner 4)                  |
| 11| ‖W‖ ≤ ‖U+V−W‖          | W not reducible by +(U+V)       | four-corner step (corner 4)                  |
| 12| ‖W‖ ≤ ‖U−V−W‖          | W not reducible by U−V          | four-corner step (corner 4)                  |

Conditions 5–12 collectively say that W is the shortest element of the coset
W + ℤU + ℤV, which is exactly what the four-corner step in
`shortenW_in_UVW` enforces.

## 4. Non-uniqueness

Minkowski reduction does **not** produce a unique reduced basis. There are
three distinct sources of ambiguity, each of which can be observed in this
code's output:

### 4.1 Sign ambiguity (always 8-fold)

If (U,V,W) is Minkowski-reduced, so is (±U, ±V, ±W) — any of the eight sign
combinations. This code makes no attempt to normalize signs; the output
sign depends on arithmetic detail and on the input. Observed in the tests:
`DeviousMat(20)` reduces to `([0,0,−1], [1,0,0], [0,1,0])` while
`DeviousMat(26)` reduces to `([1,0,0], [0,0,−1], [0,−1,0])` — both are
the same cubic basis up to sign and permutation.

### 4.2 Permutation ambiguity at tied norms

When two or three of the successive minima are equal (FCC, BCC, cubic,
hexagonal — essentially every high-symmetry Bravais lattice), several
orderings of the vectors give the same reduction. The sort step in
`minkReduce` uses `sortperm`, whose tie-breaking rule is implementation-
defined; the visible output order is therefore sensitive to the exact input.

### 4.3 Boundary ambiguity when any reduction inequality is an equality

If ‖W‖ = ‖U+V+W‖, say, then replacing W by U+V+W produces an equally
reduced basis. These boundary cases form the faces of the fundamental
polyhedral domain of Minkowski reduction in the space of 3 × 3
positive-definite matrices. On the interior of that domain (all 12
inequalities strict) the reduced basis is unique up to §4.1.

Why this is not a bug. Non-uniqueness is an intrinsic feature of
Minkowski reduction, not a property of any particular implementation. A
downstream consumer requiring a canonical form should apply a further
normalization — in crystallography this is **Niggli reduction**, which adds
sign and permutation conventions on top of Minkowski/Buerger reduction to
produce a unique cell. Selling–Delaunay reduction is the other standard.
The present package deliberately stops at Minkowski reduction; callers who
need uniqueness (e.g. for cell comparison) must post-process.

## 5. Finite-precision issues

Low-dimensional Minkowski reduction does **not** suffer the dramatic
floating-point pathologies of high-dimensional LLL, for the simple reason
that Gram–Schmidt cancellation is bounded in 2D and 3D. Nevertheless
several precision details deserve comment.

### 5.1 `floor` near integers in `shortenW_in_UVW`

The coefficients `a = floor(…)` and `b = floor(…)` can be wrong by 1 when
their argument is a near-integer floating-point value. In that case W is
translated into an adjacent parallelogram rather than the intended one,
and the four-corner test finds a corner that is not the global closest
lattice point — but is still shorter than the input W. The outer loop in
`minkReduce` then takes an extra iteration. This is not a correctness bug:
the sum ‖U‖²+‖V‖²+‖W‖² still decreases, so convergence is still guaranteed.
It is the reason an iteration limit is needed at all for floating-point
input. The fixed cap of 15 iterations is comfortable: `DeviousMat(26)`
(the worst integer instance) hits 15, but realistic floating-point
instances observed in the tests stay well below.

### 5.2 `round` at half-integer in `GaussReduce`

When `(U⋅V)/(U⋅U) ≈ ±0.5`, Julia's banker's rounding (round-half-to-even)
can go either way, producing two different but equally valid reduced pairs.
This is the floating-point manifestation of the equality case in
conditions 3 and 4. Not a bug: the output is correct either way.

### 5.3 Near-parallel U and V

`denom = (U⋅U)(V⋅V) − (U⋅V)²` is the squared area of the (U,V)
parallelogram and vanishes when U ∥ V. Floating-point division then
produces Inf or NaN, which propagates to the norms; `GaussReduce`
recognizes the NaN and throws. The tests intentionally exercise this with
a W that is almost but not quite in the U–V plane, at perturbations of
10⁻¹⁵⁰ (detected) and 10⁻¹⁷⁰ (treated as degenerate). The distinction is
genuinely determined by double-precision representability of `denom`.

(Minor: the error message on line 94 reads "linearly independent" where
"linearly dependent" is meant. Doesn't affect correctness but is
confusing.)

### 5.4 Tolerance in `isMinkReduced` is scale-dependent

Each condition uses the form

```julia
if norm(U) > norm(V)+eps() …
```

where `eps()` is the machine epsilon for `Float64`, ≈ 2.22 × 10⁻¹⁶. This
is an **absolute** tolerance. For vectors of norm ∼N the floating-point
error in `norm(·)` is ∼N·eps(), which for N ≫ 1 exceeds the tolerance
and for N ≪ 1 is smaller than the tolerance. In practice the main test
suite passes — including aspect-ratio tests up to 10⁸ — because after
reduction the norm-ordering inequalities hold with large margin, not near
equality. But a near-equality configuration at large scale could fail the
validator spuriously. A scale-aware tolerance of the form
`eps(max(norm(U),norm(V),norm(W)))` (or `eps()*max(...)`) would be more
robust.

### 5.5 Integer overflow in `DeviousMat`

The pathological unimodular matrix family `DeviousMat(n)` uses
`round(Int64, (2+√3)ⁿ / …)`. For n = 27 the entries exceed the `Int64`
range (≈ 9.2 × 10¹⁸) and silently overflow in recent Julia versions. The
comment in `runtests.jl` (formerly `@test_throws InexactError`) documents
this. Not an algorithmic concern — just a limit of the test harness.

### 5.6 Docstring example discrepancy

The `minkReduce(U,V,W)` docstring jldoctest shows the expected output as a
3-tuple, but the function returns a 4-tuple including the iteration count.
Documenter.jl will fail this doctest if it runs. The `runtests.jl` tests
correctly check all four return values.

### 5.7 What is *not* a problem

- **Cancellation in `shortenW_in_UVW`'s algebraic projection.** The
  expressions are polynomial in the dot products; no subtraction of
  nearly equal large quantities occurs in the dimensions of interest.
- **Integer inputs.** When the input is integer, all dot products are
  exact (modulo `Int64` range), and `floor`/`round` are applied to exact
  rationals. There is no precision issue at all.
- **The `≈` stop in `GaussReduce`.** As discussed at length in §2.1, this
  looks like a cosmetic tolerance check but is what prevents the
  algorithm from oscillating forever on inputs with `‖U‖ = ‖V‖`. Its
  `isapprox` default relative tolerance of `√eps` (≈ 1.5 × 10⁻⁸) is
  well-suited to 2D where the norm ratios involved are either ≈ 1 (the
  termination case we want to catch) or bounded away from 1 by more than
  that threshold; there is no risk of stopping too early.
- **The matrix-form `minkReduce(M)` dropping the iteration count.** This
  is a deliberate API choice — the iteration count is only meaningful in
  the three-vector form.

---

## 6. Summary

The implementation in this repository is a faithful and correct realisation
of the Nguyen–Stehlé (2004) greedy algorithm specialised to three
dimensions. Its mathematical correctness is robust: the outer-loop
convergence and the four-corner step are both proved by geometric
arguments that survive small floating-point perturbations. The only
precision risks are

1. the scale-blind tolerance in `isMinkReduced`,
2. a cosmetic error-message mistake in `GaussReduce`, and
3. a stale jldoctest in the `minkReduce` docstring.

Non-uniqueness of the output is fundamental to Minkowski reduction and
will not be resolved by any change to this code; callers needing a
canonical basis should post-process with Niggli reduction (e.g. via a
crystallographic library). See `plan.md` for suggested concrete changes.
